Evidence, not claims

Impact & evidence

Forkcast's public-benefit case — better nutrition transparency for restaurant meals, especially at the independent restaurants federal menu-labeling rules don't reach — will be made with measured evidence. This page defines the measurements before the pilot, and reports nothing that hasn't happened.

Standing commitment. No users, restaurants, partners, outcomes, revenue, or health effects are claimed here or anywhere in the product unless they are recorded, dated, and reproducible. The current catalog is demonstration data; orders are prototype orders; every simulated state is labeled simulated.

Pilot metrics — pre-registered

Defined now, measured when the Boston independent-restaurant pilot runs. Definitions won't move after the fact.

Pre-order decision rate

Defined · not yet measured

Share of logged restaurant meals whose dish was viewed in Forkcast before the order was placed.

Why it matters: Measures whether the product changes the decision, not just records it.

Verified-menu coverage

Defined · not yet measured

Percent of listed dishes whose nutrition data has been reviewed and confirmed by the restaurant.

Why it matters: The transparency gap for independent restaurants is the core problem.

Correction turnaround

Defined · not yet measured

Median time from a flagged nutrition value to a versioned, restaurant-approved correction.

Why it matters: Trust depends on how fast bad data gets fixed, visibly.

Logged-meal accuracy

Defined · not yet measured

Difference between order-confirmed meal contents (ground truth) and engine estimates, per nutrient.

Why it matters: The order-to-log loop creates a validation dataset no retrospective logger can build.

Independent-restaurant onboarding cost

Defined · not yet measured

Hours and dollars to digitize, estimate, and verify one independent restaurant's full menu.

Why it matters: National importance requires a protocol that scales beyond one city.

Diet-quality delta

Defined · not yet measured

Change in sodium and calorie intake per restaurant meal for active users vs their own baseline.

Why it matters: The public-benefit claim must eventually rest on measured behavior change.

How this product accumulates evidence

Dated, tagged releases with changelogs. Orders stamped as prototype so demo data can never masquerade as traction. Meal-log entries that carry source, order reference, portion, and two confidence dimensions — exportable as JSON. Menu corrections that are versioned and public. Pre-registered pilot metrics. Each is a by-product of using the product, not a claim written after the fact.
